About Me

Hi! I'm Khadeejah, a Research Assistant at the Space and Astrophysics Research Lab (SARL) in Islamabad, Pakistan. My research mainly lies in the field of Gamma-ray Bursts (GRBs), particularly propagation and relativistic effects in ejecta. I am also interested in numerical methods of studying outflows from Active Galactic Nuclei (AGNs).

Research

High-latitude Emission in Gamma-ray Bursts: studying propagation effects
Relativistic outflows in Gamma-ray Bursts (GRBs) are collimated into jets with narrow angles due to the beaming effect. In simple kinetic models, these jets are composed of relativistic, emitting shells with spherical geometry causing a propagation effect: emission that abruptly stops results in the observed flux decaying with time, rather than immediately ceasing. We study the propagation effect in isolation by generating random, uniform emission from the surface of a static shell (click here to see how we achieved this. We then simulate emission profiles and study the corresponding observed profiles. Time-varying emission is seen to be delayed by a time scale that is consistent with the angular spreading time scale found in literature. In particular, we find that the presence of flux variations in the observed profile depends on the size of the emission region relative to the time scale of pulses in the emission profile.
An initial paper has been submitted to the ICASE 2021 conference in Islamabad, Pakistan.

Simulating the relativistic beaming effect in emission from gamma-ray burst ejecta.
In astrophysical sources with relativistic jets such as gamma-ray bursts or active galactic nuclei, a commonly encountered problem is that of transforming the luminosity from the comoving frame of reference of the jet to the observer frame of reference. Such calculations are usually carried out through Doppler transformation of quantities between the two frames of reference in the framework of radiative transfer theory. In this work, we show that equivalent results can be obtained by working with the probability density functions of photons or rays emitted at verions angles in both frames of reference. We first carry out the Doppler transformation for an isotropic emission in the comoving frame of reference and obtain well-known results obtained from tranforming the luminosity. We also extend this method to a limb-darkened emission in the comoving frame of reference to obtain the emission probability density function in the observer frame of reference.

Calculating errors in GRB spectral energy distributions (SEDs)
The application of empirical functions to the analysis of gamma-ray burst (GRB) spectra plays an important role in understanding their fundamental nature. These functions, called spectral models, are fitted to spectral energy distributions (SEDs).

We develop a software tool which calculates and plots errors in Spectral Energy Distributions (SEDs) in Gamma-ray Burst analyses. The tool inputs FITS files which contain the results of spectral fits, obtained from RMfit. In particular, the tool utilizes the covariance matrix which contains covariances of correlated parameters of a particular spectral model. The tool's output is a SED butterfly plot with complete error propagation. Click here to go to the GitHub repository for the tool.

Outreach

Community Service: As an undergrad, I realized that a long-term goal of mine was to be a science educator for children in Pakistan. I regularly volunteered at UMEED, a brilliant non-profit organisation started by university students for students from low-income families, enrolled in government schools with dismal resources. Astrophotography: My family and I love to travel to the North of Pakistan - a region canopied by the Himalayan, Karakoram, and Hindu Kush mountain ranges. By day, the mountains provide the perfect opportunity for a postcard photo but at night, far, far away from any city lights, they blend into the dark sky. You wouldn't be able to tell where the peaks end and the heavens begin, if not for the latter being completely engulfed by the Milky Way.
I drag a cousin (I'm scared of ghosts) into the black, chilly night and force him to strain his neck upward with me. Up at the only sources of illumination for miles around us, until our necks cramp up. My DSLR is pointed upward too, but thankfully the tripod can take the weight.
